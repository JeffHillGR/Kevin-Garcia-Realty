Zillow Isn't Telling You the Truth (and It's Not Even Sorry)

If Zillow were a person, it would be the friend who confidently gives directions and somehow sends you into a lake.

Don't get me wrong, Zillow is useful. I check it. You check it. Your cousin who "almost became a realtor" checks it. But treating a Zestimate like gospel is where things go sideways.

Here's the thing. Zillow doesn't see your house. It doesn't smell the fresh paint. It doesn't know you replaced the roof last year or that your neighbor collects rusty lawn sculptures. It doesn't feel the awkward layout, the surprisingly perfect natural light, or the fact that your basement ceiling is doing its best.

Zestimates are based on algorithms, not emotions, and real estate is deeply emotional. Algorithms don't understand that buyers will pay more for a home that feels right, or less for one that reminds them of their aunt's house in 1997.

I've seen Zestimates miss the mark by tens of thousands of dollars, both high and low. Overpricing because of an inflated Zestimate can cause a listing to sit, and nothing scares buyers like a house that's been lingering too long. Underpricing leaves money on the table, which hurts even more.

Think of Zillow like a weather app. Helpful for a general forecast, but you still look outside before deciding what to wear.

If you want a real value, you need a real person. Someone who knows your market, your street, your buyer pool, and how homes are actually performing, not how an algorithm guesses they might.

Zillow isn't evil. It's just not your agent.
